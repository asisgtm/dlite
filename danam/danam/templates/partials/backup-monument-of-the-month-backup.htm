Monument of the Month

Welcome to our series ‘Monuments of the Month’! Each month, we select one monument that we want to highlight and bring close to you. We hope that this feature makes you curious to tap into DANAM in order to find your own ‘special sites’. Many of the MOMs have been chosen by our team members. But we also invite renowned heritage experts from Nepal and beyond, to take you to ‘their’ MOM’s. Please stay tuned and watch out for our next Monument of the Month! We also welcome your suggestions.

September 2021 - The Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā (built in 1750 CE) || सा:ग: मन्द: फल्चा(DANAM ID: BKT0100)

Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā, view from E; photo by Bijay Basukala; 2021-08-11
Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā, view from SE; photo by Bijay Basukala; 2021-08-11
Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā, view from S; photo by Bijay Basukala; 2021-08-11

The Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā, located at the Sāgaḥ Ṭola in Kirtipur, is a very particular form of arcaded platform. For one, it is free-standing and square, with 16 pillars on which the roof rests. Because of this, it is also called the Sorhakhuṭṭe “sixteen-legged” Phalcā. Moreover, because the square structure is said to contain nine maṇdaḥs, it is also known as Mandaḥ Phalcā. Another unique feature is that a water tank for drinking water - jaḥdhuṃ - is placed on the platform under the roof. Normally, such a tank could be found next to, but not in a building. Below the jaḥdhuṃ's outlet is a statue of Bhagīratha. A śivaliṅga is placed right behind the jaḥdhuṃhiti.

Built in 1750 CE, the Sāḥgaḥ Mandaḥ Phalcā was constructed in memory of a deceased father from an Amatya family from the Dathu Ṭola. Probably, it collapsed during the Great Earthquake of 1934 CE. Since then, only a square plinth along with the śivaliṅga and the jaḥdhuṃ remained until, in 2008, the Department of Archaeology reconstructed the Phalcā under the supervision of Sukra Sagar Shrestha (Shokoohy and others 2014, p. 162).

Pilgrims used to take the road in front of the Phalcā for their pilgrimage to the Mātātīrtha on Mātātīrtha Aũśī (the new moon day of the dark fortnight of Vaiśākha). Until the late 1970s, ocal youths used to fill up the jaḥdhuṃ to provide drinking water to the pilgrims and also ffered them pāṃti (a mixture of water and coarse brown sugar) on a particular day. This radition ended in the 1980s. The road also used to be a section of the trade route for the erchants of Citlāṅga (Chitlang) who travelled to Kathmandu and Patan via Kirtipur until the ate Rāṇā period. Travelers and merchants presumably used to take shelter at this Phalcā and, n this context, quenched their thirst from the jaḥdhuṃ.

August 2021 - Vatsalā Mandira (DANAM ID: BKT0100)

The deity Vatsalā is considered one of the Tantric manifestations of goddess Parvatī. She is referred to as the clan deity of the Malla Kings in an inscription (now lost) in Bhaktapur. There are three temples dedicated to Vatsalā in the small perimeter of the Bhaktapur Darbar Square. Among them, this south-facing Temple in discussion is located in the centre of the square and is said to be built by King Bhūpatīndra Malla in 1699 CE, i.e., NS 820 (cp. Nepālikabhūpavaṃśāvalī 19.1-73). It stands out because of its artistic merits and is entirely constructed in stone, except for the pinnacle. The Temple’s outer structure rests on 12 columns and has a three-level plinth. A bell supported by ornately designed columns and lintel rests on the lowest plinth and a pair of elephants and lions flank the entrance to the sanctum. Instead of a statue, a stone kalaśa as the representation of the Tantric deity Vatsalā is consecrated inside the sanctum. Most of the Vatsalā temples in the Kathmandu Valley have such aniconic stones to represent her. The local community believes that the deity was offered human sacrifice as part of the Tantric rites (Gutschow 2014, 33). The Devamālāvaṃśāvalī mentions that such rites were performed while consecrating goddess Vatsalā and establishing her shrine in Pashupati (cited from M. Thāpā VS 2077, 183). Furthermore, residents of the area also believe that dogs start crying when the Temple bell is rung. In the Vatsalā Temple at Pashupati, dogs are said to be taken inside the sanctum during some rituals, and members of a certain guṭhī howl like wolves while kneeling and praying. There appear to be some connections between these rituals and strongly held local beliefs. The Temple’s structure, except its plinth, crumbled down in the 2015 CE earthquakes, and the Bhaktapur Municipality completed its renovation in 2020 CE (VS 2077). July 2021 - The Vajra Gaṇeśa Mandira (DANAM ID: BAL0004)

The Vajra Gaṇeśa Mandira (BAL0004) is located in Lāchi Ṭola, Balambu. The west-facing Temple is built on a rectangular base and has a phalcā like structure, i.e. its three sides are open and twenty wooden columns support its roof. It is very likely that this monument was previously a phalcā and later converted into a temple. Although the reason(s) behind such a conversion is not clear, it shows how the original structure and usages of cultural heritage sites change in due course of time. This sets a good example for why (in)tangible heritage should be preserved physically and digitally. Without proper documentation, the historical origins and transformations of such monument sites will remain unrecorded. To address these issues, since 2018, the Nepal Heritage Preservation Project is focusing on ‘digital preservation’ of the cultural heritage of the Kathmandu Valley and beyond. For more information about this monument, visit our database here.

June 2021 - Tashi Thongmon Gumbā in Solukhumbu (DANAM ID: SOL0010)

Tashi Thongmon Gumbā in Junbesi (Dudhkunda, Solukhumbu) is considered to be the oldest Sherpa monastery. Junbesi was the first permanent settlement of the Sherpa people after they crossed over from Tibet in the early 16th century. The original monastery may date to that time. Other sources mention 1635 as the construction date. The monastery was damaged by an earthquake in the 17th century and partly destroyed by fire in 1914. Recent renovations include the replacement of the timber-framed entry porch with a cement and concrete portico. In 2007, the historic courtyard building was demolished and rebuilt as a stone and concrete structure covered with a corrugated metal roof.

The three massive clay statues of Buddha Śākyamuni, Maitreya and Padmasambhava are replacements that were made after the 1914 fire. Local lore has it that the original statues escaped the fire by flying away to an undisclosed location. The upstairs room features a niche with a group of smaller statues representing Buddha Śākyamuni, Maitreya, Padmasambhava, Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana amongst others. The Gumbā houses a set of the Kangyur and a set of the Tengyur.

The Gumbā is privately owned by a number of Sherpa families but looked after by the Serlo Monastery. A resident monk is deputed to arrange for necessary rituals and opens the Gumbā for visitors and pilgrims. The monk also runs a small monastery gift shop in the new building. During the yearly Dumje festival the birth of Guru Rinpoche is celebrated and dances performed in the courtyard. Please visit the database DANAM for a detailed documentation of this monument.

May 2021 - Sarāḥ Phalcā (LAL4198)

One of the most distinct and yet often overseen rich and lived architectural elements of Newari intangible and tangible heritage is the phalcā (Newari) or pāṭī (Nepali). It is an arcaded platform with multiple functions for social gatherings, inviting elderly people to spend their leisure time sitting and chatting, men of all ages to play cards, read newspapers or deposit temporarily loads, children to play. Pāṭīscan be used for religious purposes in a neighbourhood, such as playing bhajanas (devotional songs) in the mornings and evenings of particular auspicious days, and some selected ones become crucial for the distribution of ritual food such as samaybaji, devotional singing or community assembling during the religious chariot processions (e.g., Indra Yātrā, Buṅgadyaḥ Yātrā, see LAL4198). Once a new chariot is constructed, fragments of the old ones are placed in front of selected pāṭīs and worshipped.

The history of the phalcā goes back to the Licchavi period, but their use, and presence has changed over time, and with socio-spatial, demographic dynamics. Whether endangered by encroachment or demolition, whether cherished or even rejuvenated depends on the community/locality caring for the phalcā. Though partly endangered, the phalcā is not on its ‚way out’. Even today, communities invest in the construction of new pāṭīs.

DANAM LAL4198:
Sarāḥ Phalcā stands out for its remarkable pillar carvings. Located in Ikhachen (Ikhācheṃ), to the east of Tapahiti, the original name of the Phalcā is Sadāvarta Phalcā, most likely because it was constructed under the Sadāvarta Guṭhī of Śreṣṭhas from Ikhachen. No evidence could be found with which to date its initial construction. However, relying upon the wood carvings of the monument, it appears that at least the pillars were constructed during the late Malla period. Until the early 2000s, a library was kept in the Sarāḥ Phalcā, but it was closed down. The Phalcā is in severely endangered as it currently lacks a roof (covered by a large tarpaulin). This was removed and has not yet been replaced, meaning that the Phalcā is not appropriately protected from the heavy monsoon rains. In November 2019, several of its side walls were dismantled and rebuilt.

DANAM SUN4210:

Dāphā Phalcā, also called Cībhāḥ Phalcā, is located at the side of a busy road in the small town of Sunaguthi (or Sunakothi). It has finely carved pillars, decorative cornices, and a three-bay window. However, it is in a very neglected state and is missing its original roof. The roof currently consists of corrugated metal sheets supported by a make-shift structure of crudely shaped sticks. The window frames are damaged and sections of the carved frames are missing. This once free-standing Phalcā is further encroached upon by neighbouring private buildings. The floor and walls of the Phalcā are covered with cement plaster, which has led to the spread of damp. Two plywood door panels provide a way to the back room and have been inserted into poorly carved frames.

The Phalcā is mainly related to the Caitya which stands in front of it. During the anniversary celebration (varṣavarddhanapūjā) of the Caitya and other ritual worships, people use the building as a resting place. The local Jyāpu-community chants devotional songs at the Phalcā during the months of Gũlā (Śrāvaṇa-Bhādra) and Kārtika. According to a local elder, the Nāmasaṅgīti is also recited here during some of the festivals. On the 8th day of the bright fortnight of Śrāvaṇa, local residents from Sunaguthi offer religious gifts to their Vajrācārya priests at the Phalcā.

DANAM LAL4212

This unusually shaped Phalcā dates back to the first half of the 19th century. It has been converted into two small shops for some decades. The rents support two different Guṭhīs from nearby Ṭolas. 

April 2021 - Lamjī Pañcadevala (DLK0320)

The śikhara temples of Lamjī stand as unique monuments in the history of west Nepal’s architecture. The temples are built following the concept of a pañcāyatana (“five altars”) setting, thus creating a pañcadevala plan (devala is the term used in western Nepal for śikhara temples). While śikharas are usually constituted of a curvilinear tower (the actual śikhara), the tower of the central temple in Lamjī Pañcadevala is rectilinear. Elsewhere, this feature is only seen at the Dewal Bajh temple in Bajhang District (Sharma 2012: 241, Pl. 65a). Another unique feature of the site is the presence of a finely carved śivaliṅga on top of the northeastern śikhara. This śivaliṅga was most probably moved from one of the temples’ sanctum to its current location. The rectilinear tower of the central temple is an interesting indicator of the creativity of medieval builders of western Nepal, who produced variations on a well-established type of architecture. Thus, the Lamjī Pañcadevala appears as an important historical site for the study of Nepalese architecture.

Please visit the Digital Archive of Nepalese Arts and Monuments for more information about the Lamjī Pañcadevala and other west Nepal monuments.

March 2021 - Karnali bridges

Bridges made of stone and wood punctuate the landscape of the Karnali region. Especially found in the Jumla District of West Nepal, these monuments are locally known as sā̃ghu. They are built on the cantilever principle that is met in other Himalayan regions. The Jumli bridges stand out for the unique sculptural decorations found on vertical posts, with iconographies ranging from images of soldiers with rifles, planes, protectors or geometric shapes. Usually not older than fifty years, their cyclical construction requires large trees (a material more and more rare to procure) and a special knowledge of the technology. Although recently built suspension bridges are sometimes built next to them, traditional bridges often remain the preferred mode of crossing. One of the aims of the NHDP project is to document these bridges and the knowledge of their builders, in order to safeguard it for future generations.

For more information about such bridges, please visit the DANAM database.

February 2021 - Chusyā Bāhāḥ

Chusyā Bāhāḥ, located in Jyāthā Ṭola, is one of the oldest bāhāḥs in Kathmandu, which still retains its original architecture. There is some debate about the Bāhāḥ's initial construction date. Karel Rijk van Kooij (1977, 39-82) is of the opinion that the iconography and stylistic details of the roof brackets are concurrent with drawings found in 11th century manuscripts.  Shantaram Balchandra Deo (1969, 10-11) has dated the struts to the 14th century

The earliest dated sources related to the Bāhāḥ are two stone inscriptions issued during the reign of King Pratāpa Malla (r. 1641-74): A nicely preserved inscription from NS 788 (1668 CE) and a damaged inscription, most probably issued before NS 788 (1668 CE). The physical and palaeographical features of these inscriptions suggest that they were issued by the same person within a period of a few years.

The NS 788 (1668 CE) inscription records the consecration of Guṇākara Mahāvihāra (Chusyā Bāhāḥ) ‘guṇākara mahāvihāra pratiṣṭhā saṃpūrṇa yāṅana’ and the installation of Harāhara Lokeśvara sponsored by a person named Guṇajyoti (text: Guṇajoti) Vajrācārya together with his family members from Dhvākhā Bāhāḥ; the damaged inscription mentions the completion of the Bāhāḥ ‘thva vihara dayakā’.

A third inscription installed inside the sanctum of the Bāhāḥ also by Guṇajyoti in NS 769 (1649 CE) records the renovation of a rest-house (capāḥ) constructed by his father Jñānajyoti (text: Jñānajoti) in honour of their clan deity, Maricī Devī. This indicates that there was a shrine or a pīṭha (abode) dedicated to Maricī Devī nearby. Chusyā Bāhāḥ is not mentioned in this inscription. If the expansive Mahāvihāra had existed here during this time, it would probably have been mentioned in the inscription which records the family tree of Guṇajyoti and the renovation of the capāḥ.

Thus, it is likely that the broken inscription was issued after NS 769 (1649 CE). We can then conclude that the Bāhāḥ must have been constructed sometime after NS 769 (1649 CE), and was consecrated (probably after its renovation) in NS 788 (1668 CE). One could even argue that Guṇajyoti converted the capāḥ built by his father into a bāhāḥ and named it Guṇākara Mahāvihāra (Chusyā Bāhāḥ) after him. Chusyā Bāhāḥ's saṅgha members believe that Guṇajyoti constructed the Bāhāḥ by spending his income generated from his trade in Tibet.

For more information about Chusyā Bāhāḥ, please visit the DANAM database (KAT0160 Chusyā Bāhāḥ)
